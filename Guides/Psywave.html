How Psywave works... and sometimes fails

Introduction

Psywave is, frankly, one of the oddest moves in Pokémon history. It's a set damage Psychic-type move akin to Seismic Toss, only the damage is between 1 and 1.5* the user's level. This equation has changed quite a bit over the years, but we'll be focusing on RBY's today. As a result of this equation, at L100, you can deal between 1 and 150 damage to the opponent. However, this also means that around 2/3 of the time, you're dealing equal or less damage than if you used Seismic Toss, which is partly why it's so frowned upon, in addition to the fact it's only 79.7% accurate, considering the 1/256 miss. Did you know that this was Mewtwo's signature move in the Pokémon Adventures manga, by the way? Another fun fact, Psywave's animation also doesn't shake the whole screen!

Despite the move's pressing issues in regards to viability, however, Psywave has been a source of contention on the policy front in competitive RBY. Let's get into the nitty-gritty.

The Problem

When I said that Psywave deals damage between 1 and 1.5* the user's level, this was a half-truth. It's true for in-game battles and the Stadium games, as well as one side of a Link Battle. However, the other side of a cartridge link battle will have the damage be between 0 and 1.5* the user's level. Thus, on one side the target will take 1 damage, the on the other side the target will take 0 damage. As you would imagine, this causes the two games to desynchronize and effectively end the game there and then.

The desync, given its nature, becomes more common the lower the Psywave user's level is. Here is a table showing the chances of it occurring, courtesy of Peasounay and the RBY Council on Smogon. The chance for a desync can be up to 25% with normally obtainable levels, before eventually lowering to 0.662%, or 1/151, at L100. As a result, should a player want to trigger the desync, the best way is to get a Pokémon with a level as low as possible. An L3 Jigglypuff can be caught just outside Mt. Moon, providing the maximum legitimate desync chance of 20%. Technically, since Jigglypuff only requires an evolution stone to evolve, Wigglytuff can also achieve this desync chance. With tradebacks, more Pokémon get access to Psywave by breeding, such as Koffing, allowing it to be used at L5 with a 12.5% chance of causing the desync.

In terms of competitive use that isn't with the intention of causing a Desync, Butterfree would be the most notable user, though it's obviously not a great Pokémon on its own. Due to its already poor damage output, Butterfree makes use of Psywave for its set damage properties, which can situationally make up for it. This can be seen in Gen 1 Random Battles on Pokémon Showdown. Let's say we fight a Pokémon with 100 HP and 100 Special that's hit neutrally by Psychic. In this case, Butterfree deals a maximum of 67 damage, or 16.6%, which is already mediocre, not considering Psychic-types that would take even less damage. With Psywave, Butterfree would actually deal more damage at least 2/3 of the time, so overall, Butterfree fares better with it in this instance.

The Policy

Due to the potential for a desync, Psywave was banned on Pokémon Perfect in 2017, and it wasn't until later when the ban would be questioned. This was due to the arrival of more effective battle simulators and the ability to customize the implementations, namely Pokémon Showdown. The process of patching moves began with the RBY Counter Vote, though its validity is contested due to the circumstances behind it. Regardless, this put the wheels in motion for patching thaws and Psywave, allowing the latter to finally be unbanned. I personally patched Psywave on Pokémon Showdown after a few months of it not being done, calling it the "Desync Clause Mod". With this, in the event of a desync, the clause will activate and make the move fail. The first replay with it working can be seen here. Considering the 1/256 miss and the 0.662% chance of a Desync, with Desync Clause Mod in play, Psywave is 79.038% accurate for an L100 Pokémon.

However, the concept of patching RBY in general has been of significant debate among the community. Prominent voices believe that patching desyncs is bad for cartridge accuracy, which is the spirit of a battle simulator, and that anything that could cause a desync should be banned by this principle. Others argue that due to the potential to find desyncs in the future, as well as ones that could cause big bans like the thaw desync, it's more efficient to patch them. In fact, we conducted a survey for our own tiering, which showed patching as the majority's first choice, with bans coming a close second.

Conclusion

Psywave can be considered to be one of the worst moves in RBY from many perspectives: policy, competitive use, and even in-game use. There's virtually no reason to ever use it in a real game, except for maybe Random Battles. Even if you do try to use it in a competitive battle, every use can potentially mean a desync. It's frankly astonishing. That's all for today, though. Maybe leave your thoughts in the RBY 2k20 Discord!
